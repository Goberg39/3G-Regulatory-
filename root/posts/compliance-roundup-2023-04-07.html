April 7, 2023

Compliance Roundup

CFPB Defines “Abusive,” NY Proposes Cybersecurity Changes, RON in Massachusetts, Wells Adds to “Grand Slam” of Enforcement

Thanks for tuning in to our summary of the top legal and compliance news from the past week.

Top Stories

CFPB Issues Policy Statement Defining "Abusive"

Since Dodd-Frank added the concept of "abusive" conduct in 2010, industry participants have frequently criticized the CFPB for failing to provide meaningful guidance on what the standard actually meant. This week, the Bureau attempted to address that concern by issuing formal guidance describing how it interprets abusive acts and practices.

According to the CFPB, conduct may be abusive when it materially interferes with a consumer's understanding of financial products or takes unreasonable advantage of a consumer's lack of knowledge, bargaining power, or reliance on a financial institution.

While the guidance does not radically change compliance expectations, it provides useful examples institutions can apply when evaluating UDAAP risk.

New York Proposes More Cybersecurity Changes

The New York Department of Financial Services released a proposed second amendment to its cybersecurity regulations. Proposed changes would increase reporting obligations, require stronger controls for remote access, and expand cybersecurity program requirements.

Financial institutions should expect regulators to continue raising expectations around cyber risk management and data security governance.

Massachusetts Permanently Approves Remote Online Notarization

Massachusetts joined the growing list of states permitting Remote Online Notarization. Beginning in 2024, notaries will be permitted to perform remote notarizations under permanent state authority.

This is particularly noteworthy because Massachusetts has traditionally maintained attorney involvement requirements during residential real estate closings.

FinCEN Warns About Email Compromise in Real Estate Transactions

FinCEN released a report analyzing business email compromise fraud within the real estate industry. The agency identified title companies, settlement agents, lenders, and consumers as frequent targets because of the significant wire transfers associated with residential transactions.

Fraudsters continue exploiting email systems to redirect settlement proceeds and closing funds. FinCEN emphasized rapid reporting as a critical element of recovering stolen funds.

Other News

  • Virginia passed legislation expanding remote work options for mortgage lenders and brokers.
  • FDIC announced additional focus on appraisal bias during examinations.
  • Federal regulators continue emphasizing fair lending concerns involving appraisal practices.
  • Industry attention remains focused on cybersecurity and privacy developments.

Wells' Woes

Yet another enforcement action was announced against Wells Fargo, this time involving sanctions compliance failures and inadequate oversight of software used by a European banking partner.

Wells was criticized for continuing to allow use of systems despite repeated warnings involving transactions tied to sanctioned parties and jurisdictions.

"There is no way any company will ever match Wells Fargo for its record of misconduct, criminal and civil enforcement, and described Wells Fargo's latest enforcement issue as yet another addition to its "Grand Slam of Enforcement."
