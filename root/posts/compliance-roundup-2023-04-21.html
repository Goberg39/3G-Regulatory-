April 21, 2023

Compliance Roundup

CFPB Data Breach; Bill Proposed to Prohibit Trigger Leads; State Licensing Updates; Fair Lending Settlement on LGBTQ Grounds

Thanks for tuning in to our summary of the top legal and compliance news from the past week.

Top Stories

CFPB Data Breach Exposes Consumer Information

The CFPB disclosed that a former employee improperly forwarded information relating to more than 250,000 consumer accounts to a personal email account.

According to the Bureau, most of the information involved transaction-specific account identifiers rather than actual account access credentials. Nevertheless, the size of the breach resulted in substantial scrutiny from lawmakers and raised questions regarding the CFPB’s handling of sensitive information.

The timing was particularly unfortunate because the CFPB was simultaneously expanding data collection requirements in other areas of consumer lending.

Arizona Mortgage Company Settles LGBTQ Discrimination Allegations

A small Arizona mortgage lender agreed to resolve allegations of discrimination involving sexual orientation. Although the publicly available information provides limited detail about the factual circumstances, regulators emphasized that lending decisions must be based on neutral underwriting criteria and not protected characteristics.

The matter serves as another reminder that regulators continue expanding their focus on fair lending concerns across a broad range of consumer populations.

State Licensing Developments Continue

Several states announced changes to mortgage licensing requirements and remote-work policies.

  • Virginia expanded remote-work flexibility for licensed mortgage companies.
  • Arkansas modified sponsorship requirements for loan officers.
  • North Dakota updated lender licensing requirements.
  • California proposed additional registration and reporting clarification.

One important takeaway is that a loan officer must remain compliant with the licensing requirements of every state in which they conduct activity. The fact that one state permits remote work does not necessarily mean another state does.

Congress Considers Trigger Lead Legislation

Congress introduced legislation intended to prohibit the creation and sale of mortgage trigger leads.

Supporters argue that trigger leads create confusion, generate excessive solicitations during the mortgage process, and increase opportunities for consumer deception and fraud. Industry groups voiced support for the proposal.

Some consumers reportedly received dozens of calls, emails, and text messages within hours of beginning the mortgage application process.

Florida Loan Officer Convicted of Mortgage Fraud

A Florida loan officer was convicted of bank fraud and identity theft after prosecutors alleged she fabricated family court documents, forged judicial signatures, and created fictitious child-support records in order to qualify borrowers who otherwise would not have met underwriting standards.

According to court filings, false income was created through fabricated child-support histories, fake court orders, and manufactured supporting documentation.

Other News

  • CFPB announced a hearing focused on so-called "zombie second mortgages."
  • Litigation against Rocket Mortgage involving refinance rate-lock representations continued.
  • CFPB continued advocating for a broader interpretation of ECOA protections.

Wells' Woes

Regulators concluded that audit leadership repeatedly failed to identify, escalate, or properly respond to warning signs associated with the bank's sales-practices scandal despite receiving thousands of consumer complaints, internal alerts, media reports, and fraud concerns.

Particularly striking was the allegation that serious misconduct concerns were repeatedly brought to management's attention, yet audit reports continued to conclude that controls were functioning effectively.

OCC investigators reportedly asked whether anything had ever received an unsatisfactory audit rating during the relevant period. The response: "Nothing I can think of."
